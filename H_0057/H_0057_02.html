Political History Collection

Interview H.0057.02 : Tape 2

: with Gyalo Thondup [Tib. rgya lo don grub]

Melvyn Goldstein, Editor

Center for Research on Tibet
Department of Anthropology

Case Western Reserve University
Cleveland, Ohio

©2005

Location of Interview: Hong Kong, China
Date of Interview: 14 April 1995
Language of Interview: The interview was conducted, recorded, and transcribed in English.
Interview by: Melvyn Goldstein
Name: Gyalo Thondup [Tib. rgya lo don grub]
Gender: Male
Age: 67
Date of Birth: 1928

Abstract

Gyalo Thondup [Tib. rgya lo don grub] is an older brother of the Dalai Lama. In this interview he discusses some issues in the modern political history of Tibet. He also discusses his disappointed with the Chinese leadership in Lhasa in 1952 regarding the way that they behaved and their policies. He then describes his attempts at getting India to help by letting the Dalai Lama come to India and by helping to publicize the events occurring in Tibet. Additionally, he discusses Taiwan’s role in Tibet and the training given by the U.S., the arms that they dropped and their thoughts about providing more aid. Finally, he talks about some of the current political issues between China and Tibet.

Tape 2

Q: I've seen a lot of Indian documents through various sources, not complete, though, and I know that you and Shakabpa and khenjung were working, talking to the Indians. And I understand the reason why you came out from Lhasa back to India to bring the Tibetan issue up and try to get help from the various governments. But it seems to me that the Indians were being very cooperative. There was the Sikkim Political Officer, Aba Pant, and then the C.I.D., there was Mr. Bose Kaul and some people like that, and there was... I've seen all kinds of documents talking about giving wireless training for people and this is not the U.S., I'm saying the Indians. And giving so much money every month, not much, a thousand, five hundred rupees for some of these training. But I only have individual things, I don't have the whole picture. So up until the Dalai Lama came in 56-57, it sounds like there was a lot of progress and that you had a plan as to what to do, and the Dalai Lama to come, and there was cooperation with the Indians, and then of course Nehru changed his mind. If you could tell me a little bit in detail as to what went on, how it proceeded, and what the goals were, or if I'm wrong?

A: [Editor's note: the interviewee's English has not been corrected] I see. No, I think you're right But about what you call... my purpose coming out from Lhasa in 1952 to India was mainly because I was very much disappointed with the behavior and the policies of the Chinese leadership in Lhasa. The General Zhang Jingwu and Zhang Guohua, and Tan Guansan. It was shocking, I mean the excuses during my stay in 1952. Which I returned from the United States anticipating maybe that the communist government would be treating the Tibetans in a reasonable manner. But during my stay in those months I was really shocked and disappointed. And then the situation deteriorated because you probably also heard of the so-called, the People's Association, "Mimang Movement." Of course people called it "mimang," I mean "mimang" means just "people." Those days hundreds and hundreds of petitions were coming from all over Tibet to the Tibetan government, that means the Sitsab Lukhangwa, [Tib. klu khang ba] and Lobsang Tashi [Tib. blo bzang bkra shis] was there, and then of course Kashag, and then on the other side the Chinese authorities, the military authorities. Petitions appealing for withdrawing Chinese troops, and complaining about the inflation. The food prices really very much terribly increased compared to... I'll just give you some instances: like normally we buy one, say, for meat, for the whole sheep, to butcher the sheep, I think we paid at that time, before the Chinese came in, we paid about, say 30-40 sang. The sang is the equivalent of about five rupees or something like that. After the Chinese reached Lhasa, and waited a few months it went up to 200-300 sang. That means about 40-50 rupees. 30-40-50 rupees, like that. And then of course grain, like tsampa is also just flying up. So I think because of that reason the majority of the Tibetan population was very much against so many troops. And then, that's the one main reason for the inflation. The second reason, the Chinese troops were treating the Tibetans like their enemies. They move and travel with sten guns and with rifles. I mean even in the day time, even when there is a very peaceful manner everywhere, and they just ran, or occupied, like occupation, military occupation and sometimes during my travel between India to Lhasa, and I experienced some of those Chinese troops coming in the middle of the night.
